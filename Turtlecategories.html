Types of turtles in Sri Lanka



There are 250 kinds of tortoises on the planet today. Sea turtles, which are seven of these, are marine life. The remaining 243 species are terrestrial or dwell in freshwater wetlands and pools. Tortoises are the name for turtles that only exist on land, or native turtles. The shores of Sri Lanka are visited by five of the world's seven varieties of marine turtles during the breeding season. Green, Hawksbill, Olive Ridley, Loggerhead, and Leatherback turtles are among them. Sri Lanka is home to one land tortoise species and three freshwater turtle types. Parker's Black Turtles are watery turtles. Soft or flap shell turtles and Sri Lanka Black Turtles. The star turtle is a type of land tortoise. Turtles and tortoises have different types of carapaces. Swimming is made simple by the turtles' brief, streamlined carapace, or upper portion of their shell.


Olive Ridley Turtle

  • The smallest type of marine turtle is the olive ridley. They have an emerald green, heart shaped exoskeleton, or carapace. These reptiles, which eat crabs, shrimp, and jellyfish, can be found in warm seas all over the globe, particularly in the Indian and Pacific Oceans.
  • This species is well-known for "arribadas," in which a large number of tortoises congregate to lay their eggs at the same moment. Between September and November, olive ridley turtles lay their eggs on a variety of beaches in Sri Lanka, including Kosgoda, Yala, and other sporadic places. Due to the low projected population of 800,000 olive ridley turtles, this species is only designated as endangered
Green Turtle

  • The most prevalent marine turtle species in Sri Lanka are those with shells that are brown, black, or dark green in appearance. The aquatic animal gets its name because the fat underneath the tough carapace is frequently greenish. The green turtle, which can grow to a height of 120 cm, faces off against the loggerhead turtle as the second-largest type of marine turtle. Adult turtles become vegetarian and eat only seagrasses, kelp, and algae, while juvenile turtles eat worms, jellyfish, and sponges.
  • he only enemies of the big, armored adults are people and other giant sharks. This species is considered vulnerable as a result of egg collecting, unlawful shooting, the loss of nesting locations, and fishery bycatches. Throughout the year, green turtles have been observed breeding on Sri Lanka's southern beaches, with the months of January through March being the busiest. Kosgoda and Rekawa Beach are the locations where this species is most prevalent.
Hawksbill Turtle

  • These marine turtles range in size from tiny to big, and they have a hawk-like curved, pointed snout. Their colorful and erratic shell design allows them to be readily differentiated from their relatives.
  • These turtles are omnivores, eating everything from phytoplankton to marine urchins. However, favored foods are sponges, which are toxic or disagreeable to other aquatic life. Despite having a protective carapace, this species is hunted by big fish, sharks, crocodiles, and octopuses. The young turtles are susceptible to beach crabs, shore canines, and birds, just like other turtle hatchlings.
  • Over the past century, the hawksbill number is thought to have declined. Critically Endangered is the designation given to and In Sri Lanka, the hawksbills are rarer than green turtles but can be seen nesting in Kosgoda or Bundala in the countrys South.
Loggerhead Turtle

  • Their huge skulls and reddish-brown carapace help identify these giant marine lizards. Juveniles are visible in coastal waters, while hatchlings reside on algae rafts drifting in mild currents. Loggerhead turtles move to warmer waters in the winter because they are cold-blooded and must avoid water temps below 13Â°C.
  • Loggerhead turtles can submerge for up to 7 hours without taking a breath, but they usually emerge every 15 to 30 minutes to breathe. This type of turtle consumes everything from phytoplankton to jellyfish to its own young. Sharks, killer whales, and seals are examples of predators.
  • Sea turtles designated as endangered include loggerheads. Despite being extremely uncommon in Sri Lanka, loggerhead turtles have been spotted on the shores in Kosgoda, Rekawa, and Bundala.
Leatherback Turtle

  • They can reach heights well beyond 160 centimeters and weigh over 700 kg. This species' carapace mimics leather in texture and is softer and more pliable than the green turtle's. These aquatic creatures can live in colder seas and descend to depths of up to 1000 m because of their dense coating of insulating fat
  • These enormous creatures are particularly susceptible to floating plastic waste in our seas because jellyfish are their preferred food. This turtle species is considered endangered by experts. Rarely, leatherback turtles have been spotted off the coast of Sri Lanka near Kosgoda, Mawolla, or Rekawa beach
